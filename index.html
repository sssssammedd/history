INDIGENOUS LAND RIGHTS IN AUSTRALIA

A journey toward recognition, justice, and ownership of Country.

By Sam Eddy – Year 10 History Project, Central Coast Grammar School

Personal Voice

“Here is my brutal honest opinion. I've always struggled to see the deep meaningfull connection Indigenous people have to the land. I've heard of stories where Indigenous people have tried to exploit the system. Earlier this year the Wiradyuri Traditional Owners Central West Aboriginal Corporation tried to "threaten" the Bathurst 1000 in 2025 although the threat was false they put in a claim for a land claim in the McPhillamy Park although unsuccessfull it struck me because to me that race means a lot and to have it cancelled due to Indegnous people claiming the land I'd have a really really negative view of the situation.”

Although my previous negative view, my research into this topic has given me new understanding and empathy towards why land rights is to vital to who they are. The stories I've read about protests, legal battles, communities stewarding Country has ultimately shifted my view. Land for the Aboriginal People is not only about ownership. It's about identity, culture and a living relationship. I want this project to be an invitation to young people around Australia to listen, to learn, and to act with respect. I'd invite anyone who reads this information on this website to read it with a neautral mindset, I've tried to convey how far we've come as a nation but also how far we still have to go.

My Personal Voice: Why Land Rights Matter

Giving Indigenous people land rights recognises the deep, spiritual connection between First Nations peoples and country. For Indigenous Australians, land is not just property or something you build a house on. It's identity, law, heritage and the foundation of culture and community. It is vital to who they are. They have a relationship with the land, they believe if they give to the land and take care of it then the land will pay them back with nourishment, shelter and water.

Access to Country supports language, ceremony and wellbeing. Recognising that land has a deep spiritual connection within the Indigenous community is another step toward justice and reconciliation.

When land is returned or recognised, communities can find values, rules, and ways of working with the country in culturally appropriate ways and pass knowledge to future generations further enrichening the land and culture.

"When the land is taken from us or destroyed, we feel hurt because we belong to the land and we are part of it."

–Djinyini Gondarra

The Mabo Decision: Redefining Land and Justice

How a landmark legal challenge changed the idea of ownership in Australia.

The 1992 High Court decision in the Mabo case overturned the doctrine of terra nullius and recognised Native Title. This was a pivotal turning point that paved the way for the Native Title Act 1993.

Eddie Mabo's decade-long fight culminated in the High Court's 1992 judgment in Mabo v Queensland (No 2). The Court rejected the doctrine of terra nullius, recognising that Indigenous peoples' traditional laws and customs would be granted native title rights which survive colonisation, where those rights had not been lawfully extinguished.

The decision acknowledged the continuing connection of Indigenous groups to Country and created a legal foundation for native title claims. The Native Title Act 1993 was introduced to establish a process for claiming and recognising native title, balancing competing interests in land use.

Consequences & Limitations: While Mabo was transformative, native title is subject to extinguishment by valid grants of land (e.g., freehold). Claim processes can be lengthy and expensive, and outcomes often require negotiation and agreement with state and private interests. Resource development and overlapping interests continue to generate tension.

Impact and Continuing Journey

Ongoing struggles for land justice, and the importance of connection to Country remain central.

While legal recognition has expanded, practical outcomes vary. Many communities face continued barriers to control, access, and economic opportunity.

  • Long legal processes for native title claims
  • Conflicts with development and mining interests
  • Ongoing work for cultural protection and self-determination

Has the Struggle Stalled?

Although there's been steady progress, many obstacles still lie ahead. Below are balanced viewpoints.

Yes, It has stalled

  • Bureaucratic delays in Native Title claims
  • Ongoing mining approvals over sacred sites
  • Decline in political momentum for constitutional recognition

No, Progress continues

  • Community-run land councils managing large areas
  • Youth-led movements and environmental co-management
  • Increased public awareness and legal precedents
“Progress is real, but the journey is far from over.” — Contemporary Activist, 2023
